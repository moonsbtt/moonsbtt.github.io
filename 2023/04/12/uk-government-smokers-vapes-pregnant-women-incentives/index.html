UK Government Encourages Smokers to Switch to Vapes

Posted on 2023-04-12 Edited on 2023-04-13

The UK government has announced a new world-first health initiative to encourage up to one million British smokers to switch from cigarettes to vapes. Pregnant women will be offered financial incentives to make the change as part of the government’s target to reduce the number of smokers to 5 per cent or less of the population. The scheme will give almost one in five smokers a vape starter kit, along with support to quit smoking.

In a speech, Health Minister Neil O’Brien highlighted the dangers of smoking, saying, “Up to two-out-of-three lifelong smokers will die from smoking. Cigarettes are the only product on sale which will kill you if used correctly.” The government will fund a new national “swap to stop” scheme, the first of its kind in the world.

This initiative comes as vaping is discouraged in Australia. The Australian government has vowed to crack down on the vaping black market amid concerns that a new generation is becoming addicted to nicotine. Under existing laws in Australia, nicotine vapes can only be purchased with a prescription by adults trying to quit smoking. However, there is a growing cohort of vapers among people under 18 who are buying disposable vapes on the black market that are being imported and marketed in fluorescent colours and sweet flavours.

A report released last Friday found that there was only “limited evidence” that nicotine e-cigarettes were effective in helping people quit. However, there was “substantial evidence” that e-cigarette use can increase the uptake of tobacco smoking in people who do not smoke. The push to swap cigarettes for vapes is driven by the fact that tobacco is still the highest preventable cause of death and illness in Britain.

The UK government spent £68 million ($126.5 million) in 2021-22 on local authority measures to get people to stop smoking, leading to 100,000 smokers quitting, and easing the strain on Britain’s overwhelmed National Health Service. However, British health officials have warned that vaping’s popularity among children is exposing them to chemicals whose long-term effects are unclear. Health service figures show that 9 per cent of teens aged 11-15 years in Britain had used e-cigarettes in 2021, up from 6 per cent three years prior.

To prevent the illegal sale of vapes to under-18s, the UK government will set up an enforcement squad backed by £3 million in funding.

Overall, the initiative to encourage smokers to switch to vapes is a significant step towards reducing smoking-related deaths and illnesses in the UK. However, it is important to continue to monitor the effects of vaping and ensure that appropriate measures are taken to prevent underage use.
